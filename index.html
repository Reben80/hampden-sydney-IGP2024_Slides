The Point-Joining Technique

  • Artists likely used simplified methods for complex designs, often working step-by-step, and collaborating with mathematicians when needed.
  • Practical techniques like dividing a circle into equal parts were used instead of complex math, achieving good accuracy for their designs.
  • However, the point-joining method, though useful for step-by-step constructions, has limitations for highly complex designs, making it less flexible than the polygonal technique for intricate patterns.

The Grid Method

  • The orthogonal grid is a fast way to test ideas and explore design options, but it introduces distortions due to the square cell structure.
  • After creating a design using the grid, it must be redrawn to correct distortions.
  • There are multiple methods to construct less complex designs, and the same design might be created differently in various locations.
  • The grid method is quick for exploring options but requires correction, while point-joining is accurate but inflexible. The polygonal technique is both accurate and flexible.

Polygonal Technique

  • The polygonal technique is the most historically significant method for creating Islamic geometric patterns.
  • Artists use the midpoints of polygonal tessellations to locate pattern lines, then discard the tessellation, leaving the final design.
  • This technique allows for multiple designs to be created from a single tessellation, offering flexibility and diversity in pattern creation.
  • It has been used across various regions and periods, with examples found in architecture, manuscripts, and other forms of art.
  • The polygonal technique was often kept secret to protect specialized design practices, possibly explaining the scarcity of surviving reference scrolls.
  • The Topkapi Scroll is one of the most important historical documents, showcasing 157 geometric designs and demonstrating the widespread use of the polygonal technique during the Timurid and Safavid periods.
  • Some early examples of patterns even incorporate the generative tessellation into the final design, though this became less common over time.

source: Islamic Geometric Patterns: Their Historical Development and Traditional Methods of Construction by Jay Bonner

  • Start with a tessellation: Use polygons like hexagons or octagons to create a grid. also more complex grid results in complex IGP
  • Mark key points: Identify midpoints or vertices for connecting lines, sometimes creating two lines from one point at specific angles.
  • Draw pattern lines: Connect the marked points to form geometric shapes like stars.
  • Discard the grid: Remove the underlying tessellation, keeping only the final pattern.
  • Refine and vary: Adjust lines and angles to create multiple patterns from the same tessellation.

What is Group?

 

In mathematics, a group is a set of elements combined with an operation that satisfies the following properties:

1. Closure: For any two elements \(a\) and \(b\) in the group, the result of the operation \(a \cdot b\) is also in the group.
2. Associativity: The operation satisfies \((a \cdot b) \cdot c = a \cdot (b \cdot c)\) for all elements \(a\), \(b\), and \(c\) in the group.
3. Identity Element: There exists an element \(e\) in the group such that for any element \(a\), the equation \(e \cdot a = a \cdot e = a\) holds.
4. Inverse Element: For each element \(a\), there exists an element \(a^{-1}\) such that \(a \cdot a^{-1} = a^{-1} \cdot a = e\).

Symmetries of Wallpaper Group 

  • Translations: Repeating the pattern by sliding it along two independent directions.
  • Rotations: The pattern can rotate around points by 2-fold, 3-fold, 4-fold, or 6-fold symmetry.
  • Reflections: Symmetry where the pattern reflects across mirror lines.
  • Glide Reflections: Combines reflection and translation, where the pattern shifts along the axis after reflecting.

  • 17 Wallpaper Groups: There are 17 distinct symmetry groups for two-dimensional patterns, each representing a unique way patterns can repeat.

p1
This is the simplest wallpaper group, containing only translations. The primitive unit cell and translation unit cell are the same.
p2
In addition to translations, patterns in this group have four distinct points of two-fold rotational symmetry. There are two primitive unit cells in the translation unit cell.
pm 
Patterns in this group have two distinct, simple reflections that are parallel and no rotations. There are two primitive unit cells per translation unit cell.
 


pg
Patterns in this group have two distinct glide reflections that are all parallel and no simple reflections. There are two primitive unit cells per translation unit cell.

cm
Patterns in this group have one distinct simple reflection and one distinct glide reflection, with glide reflection lines falling midway between the simple reflection lines. There are two primitive unit cells per translation unit cell.

 

pmm
Patterns in this group have simple reflections in perpendicular directions and four distinct two-fold rotation centers at the intersections of the reflection lines. There are four primitive unit cells per translation unit cell.

pmg
Patterns in this group have two distinct centers of two-fold rotation, mirror lines in one direction, and glide reflection lines perpendicular to those. The rotation centers lie on the glide reflection axes. There are four primitive unit cells per translation unit cell.
 

pgg
Patterns in this group have two centers of two-fold rotation, no simple reflections, and perpendicular glide reflections. The rotation centers do not lie on the glide reflection axes. There are four primitive unit cells per translation unit cell.

cmm
Patterns in this group have three distinct centers of two-fold rotation, mirror lines in two perpendicular directions, and glide reflection lines in two perpendicular directions. Two of the rotation centers lie at the intersections of the simple reflection axes, and one at the intersections of the glide reflection axes. There are four primitive unit cells per translation unit cell.
 

p3
Patterns in this group have three distinct centers of three-fold rotation, no simple reflections, and no glide reflections. There are three primitive unit cells per translation unit cell.
p4
Patterns in this group have one center of twofold rotation, two distinct centers of four-fold
rotation, no simple reflections, and no glide reflections. There are four primitive unit cells per translation unit cell.
 

p4m
Patterns in this group have one center of twofold rotation, two distinct centers of four-fold rotation, simple reflections in four directions, and two glide reflections. The rotation centers all lie at the intersection of the reflection axes. There are eight primitive unit cells per translation unit cell.
p4g
Patterns in this group have one center of twofold rotation, two centers of four-fold rotation, simple reflections in two perpendicular directions, and glide reflections in four directions. The two-fold rotation centers lie at the intersection of the simple reflection axes. There are eight primitive unit cells per translation unit cell.

 

p3m1
Patterns in this group have three centers of three-fold rotation, simple reflections in three directions, and glide reflections along lines halfway between the reflections. The rotation centers lie at the intersections of reflection axes. There are six primitive unit cells per translation unit cell.

p31m
Patterns in this group have three centers of three-fold rotation, with reflections and glide reflections along three directions. At least one rotation center does not lie on a reflection axis. There are six primitive unit cells per translation unit cell.
 

p6
Patterns in this group have one center each of six-fold rotation, three-fold rotation, and twofold rotation and no simple reflections and glide reflections. There are six primitive unit cells per translation unit cell.
p6m
Patterns in this group have one center each of six-fold rotation, three-fold rotation, and two-fold rotation and simple reflections in six directions and glide reflections along the six lines that lie midway between the simple reflection axes. There are 12 primitive unit cells per translation unit cell.
